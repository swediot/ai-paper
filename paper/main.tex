\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We exploit Switzerland's linguistic border between French- and German-speaking regions ---the \emph{R\"{o}stigraben}---to test whether cultural norms shape the psychological cost of overwork. Using 25 waves of the Swiss Household Panel (1999--2023), we construct a panel of employed individuals in German- and French-speaking regions and measure overwork as the gap between actual and contractual weekly hours. Individual and year fixed effects regressions reveal that overwork is significantly more psychologically costly for French-speaking workers: each additional hour beyond the contract increases post-work exhaustion by 70\%, work-life interference by 112\%, and difficulty disconnecting by 34\% in French-speaking regions (all $p < 0.02$). Overwork also reduces satisfaction with free time and work conditions significantly more for French-speaking workers. These burnout channels operate even though global life and job satisfaction show no significant cultural moderation, indicating that the welfare costs of overwork are domain-specific. The effects are persistent across demographic groups, though managers face significantly higher penalties. Our findings point to deep-seated cultural schemas that shape how overwork translates into psychological strain, independent of institutional constraints.

\end{abstract}
